Fourth Question 40 Points

Mention true or false with justification (ONLY 5 statements in-class & the rest are take-home questions):

1. "Tax incentives are considered a necessary condition but not sufficient for attracting foreign direct investments to developing countries."

TRUE

Justification: While tax incentives (such as tax holidays, accelerated depreciation, investment allowances) play an important role in attracting FDI, they alone are not sufficient. Other factors are equally or more important:

  • Political stability and rule of law
  • Infrastructure quality (transportation, energy, telecommunications)
  • Skilled labor force and education system
  • Market size and access to regional markets
  • Regulatory environment and ease of doing business
  • Macroeconomic stability (low inflation, stable currency)

2. "Although the economic advantages of tax holiday as a tax incentive, but it may have different difficulties and disadvantages in application at some cases."

TRUE

Justification: Tax holidays have economic advantages (attracting investment, promoting specific sectors) but also significant disadvantages:

  • Revenue loss for the government
  • May attract "footloose" investments that leave when the holiday expires
  • Potential for abuse and tax avoidance (restructuring businesses to qualify repeatedly)
  • Creates unfair competition between existing taxpaying firms and new tax-exempt ones
  • Difficult to target effectively — benefits may flow to investments that would have occurred anyway
  • Complexity in administration and enforcement
Note: Specific details about tax holiday difficulties are drawn from general public finance principles.

3. "According to progressive income tax, MTR is always greater than ATR."

TRUE (with qualification)

Justification (from Lecture 4 - Progressive Tax Rate):

In progressive income tax by brackets:

  • "The marginal rate of taxation eventually exceeds the average rate at all brackets of income"
  • At the first bracket (exempted income), both MTR and ATR equal zero. After that, MTR > ATR at all income levels.
  • MTR increases stepwise from bracket to bracket, while ATR rises steadily but remains below MTR

Note: This is specifically true for progressive tax by brackets. For progressive tax by classes, MTR = ATR within each class.

4. "If the income tax function is T = 0.25Y, so ATR will be greater than MTR, and this tax will affect positively social equity. (Use suitable graph)"

FALSE

Justification (from Lecture 4 - Proportional Tax Rate):

T = 0.25Y (proportional tax, no exemption)
ATR = T / Y = 0.25Y / Y = 0.25 = 25%
MTR = dT / dY = 0.25 = 25%
Therefore: ATR = MTR = 25% (EQUAL, not ATR > MTR)

This is a proportional (flat) tax where:

  • Both ATR and MTR are equal and constant at 25% for all income levels
  • It does NOT positively affect social equity because all income levels bear the same tax rate
  • A progressive tax is needed to promote social equity (from the lecture: "progressive income tax especially progressive by brackets is considered an effective tax to decrease the gap between rich people and poor people")

5. "From investor point of view, carrying loss forward is preferable than carrying loss backward."

FALSE

Justification: From the investor's point of view, carrying loss backward is generally preferable to carrying loss forward because:

  • Carrying loss backward: The investor applies the loss against previous years' taxable income and receives an immediate tax refund. The tax savings are realized NOW.
  • Carrying loss forward: The investor must wait for future profitable years to apply the loss. The tax savings are deferred to the FUTURE.
  • Due to the time value of money, receiving cash (tax refund) today is worth more than receiving it in the future.
The specific mechanics of carrying loss forward vs backward are not detailed in the provided lecture notes.

7. "There are many difficulties of applying 'Benefit Principle' for distributing tax burden."

TRUE

Justification (from Lecture 4 - Tax Burden Distribution):

The Benefit Principle faces several difficulties:

  1. Free rider problem: "Each person doesn't desire to declare his benefit received from pure public goods"
  2. Measurement difficulty: "It is very difficult to determine and to measure the marginal benefit received by individuals from pure public goods"
  3. No incentive for truthful reporting: "The individuals have little or no incentive to declare their true benefits"
  4. Limited applicability: "Only possible in very small communities, where individuals know each other"

8. "If the coefficient of excludability is 0.7 and coefficient of Rivalry is 0.5, so this service may be public service. (Use suitable graph)"

FALSE

Justification:

  • Excludability = 0.7 (highly excludable)
  • Rivalry = 0.5 (moderately rival)
  • A pure public good/service has LOW excludability and LOW rivalry
  • With high excludability (0.7) and moderate rivalry (0.5), this service is closer to a private good, not a public service

10. "According to both investment allowances, and initial depreciation as tax incentives, the asset is depreciated more than its book value."

TRUE

Justification:

  • Investment allowances: Allow the investor to deduct a percentage of the asset cost as an additional allowance on top of normal depreciation, effectively depreciating more than 100% of the asset's book value.
  • Initial depreciation: Allows a large initial deduction in the first year plus normal depreciation over the asset's life, resulting in total depreciation exceeding the original book value.
  • In both cases, the total depreciation claimed over the asset's life exceeds the book value of the asset, which is the incentive mechanism — it reduces the present value of tax payments.

A) Different Forms of Monopolistic Practices

Monopolistic practices take several forms that distort market efficiency:

  1. Price Fixing: Agreements between competitors to set prices at a certain level, eliminating competition.
  2. Market Division: Competitors agree to divide markets (by geography, customer type, or product) to avoid competing with each other.
  3. Predatory Pricing: Setting prices below cost to drive competitors out of the market, then raising prices once competition is eliminated.
  4. Tying Arrangements: Requiring buyers to purchase one product (tied product) as a condition for purchasing another product.
  5. Output Restriction: The monopolist restricts output below the competitive/efficient level to maintain higher prices.
  6. Barriers to Entry: Creating artificial barriers (through patents, exclusive contracts, or lobbying for regulations) to prevent new competitors from entering the market.
