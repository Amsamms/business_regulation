First Question 10 Points

If the vaccination for Covid-19 is produced in a competitive market, the cost of the vaccination for each vaccinated person at equilibrium point is 2000 LE. At the previous price, the percentage of the vaccinated persons is 60% of total population (100 Million). According to government estimation, the marginal external benefit of producing and consuming vaccine is 600 LE and there is no marginal external cost. The efficient level of vaccinated persons will be satisfied when the number of vaccinated persons reaches 80% of population, and at this case the new efficient price is 1700 LE.

Given Data:

  • Competitive market for Covid-19 vaccination
  • No external cost → MSC = MPC
  • Positive externality exists → MEB = 600 LE per vaccination
  • Equilibrium: Q1 = 60% × 100M = 60 Million, P1 = 2,000 LE
  • Efficient level: Q2 = 80% × 100M = 80 Million, Consumer Price (P3) = 1,700 LE

Key Relationships:

MSB = MPB + MEB (positive externality exists)
MSC = MPC (no external cost)
At Market Equilibrium: MPB = MPC → E1
At Efficient Point: MSB = MSC → E2

A) Graphical Determination of Equilibrium Quantity and Price

The market equilibrium occurs at point E1 where the demand (MPB) intersects the supply (MPC = MSC):

Equilibrium Point E1:
Quantity: Q1 = 60 Million vaccinations
Price: P1 = 2,000 LE

B) Efficient Point (Quantity & Price)

The efficient point occurs at E2 where MSB = MSC. Since MEB = 600 LE, the MSB curve lies 600 LE above MPB at every quantity.

Efficient Point E2:
Quantity: Q2 = 80 Million vaccinations
Producer Price (on supply curve): P2 = 2,300 LE
Consumer Price (after subsidy): P3 = 1,700 LE

Verification: Producer price P2 − Consumer price P3 = 2,300 − 1,700 = 600 = MEB ✓

C) Suitable Government Policy

The suitable policy is a corrective subsidy equal to MEB per unit:

Corrective Subsidy = MEB = 600 LE per vaccination

This subsidy shifts MPB upward by 600 LE to MSB. The equilibrium moves from E1 to E2, increasing vaccinations from 60M to 80M.

D) Effects of Government Intervention

  • Consumer: Pays less (1,700 LE) and consumes more (80M). Consumer surplus increases.
  • Producer: Receives more (2,300 LE) and produces more (80M). Producer surplus increases.
  • Government: Total subsidy cost = 80M × 600 = 48 Billion LE.
  • Efficiency: Eliminates the deadweight loss triangle E1E2E3 — society achieves the socially optimal level of vaccinations.

Third Question 25 Points

Mention true or false with justification (5 statements in-class, the rest are take-home questions):

1. "Applying tax holiday as a tax incentive without some constraints may contradict with achieving economic development in the society." 2 pts

TRUE

Justification: Tax holidays can contradict economic development goals if unconstrained:

  • They attract short-term, footloose investment that disappears when the holiday expires (no long-run commitment)
  • Revenue losses reduce the government's ability to fund essential public goods and infrastructure
  • Without sector or location constraints, they may direct investment toward low-priority areas
  • They can be exploited through tax avoidance schemes (e.g., profit-shifting, round-tripping)
  • Unconstrained holidays create unfair competition between existing taxpaying businesses and new tax-exempt ones

2. "There is no difference between the concept of state budget according to classical view and according to Keynesian view." 2 pts

FALSE

Justification:

Classical ViewKeynesian View
A financial document: expected revenues and expenditures, authorized by legislature A financial and economic plan: reflects government goals for growth, social equity, and stability
Role limited to financial administration Role extended to economic stabilization and redistribution
Budget must be balanced (no deficit) Budget may be in deficit to stimulate the economy during recessions

3. "If the income tax function is T = 0.25Y, so ATR will be greater than MTR, and this tax will affect positively social equity. (Use a suitable graph)" 2 pts

FALSE

Justification: With T = 0.25Y (proportional tax, no exemption):

ATR = T / Y = 0.25Y / Y = 0.25 = 25%
MTR = dT / dY = 0.25 = 25%
∴ ATR = MTR = 25% (EQUAL — the statement claims ATR > MTR, which is WRONG)

This is a proportional (flat) tax. It does NOT promote social equity because:

  • Both rich and poor pay the same 25% — no redistribution
  • Only a progressive tax (ATR < MTR) positively affects social equity

4. "From investor point of view, tax savings are the same whether under applying carrying loss forward or carrying loss backward." 2 pts

FALSE

Justification: The two mechanisms differ due to the time value of money:

  • Loss carryback: Loss is applied against past taxable income → immediate tax refund. Tax savings realized now.
  • Loss carryforward: Loss is applied against future taxable income → deferred tax reduction. Tax savings realized later.
  • Since a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future, carryback generates higher PV of tax savings than carryforward.

5. "There are different forms of government intervention in the market for solving the different reasons of market failure." 2 pts

TRUE

Justification: Market failures are diverse, requiring targeted interventions:

Market FailureGovernment Intervention
Negative ExternalitiesCorrective tax (Pigouvian tax)
Positive ExternalitiesCorrective subsidy
Monopolistic PowerAnti-trust laws, price regulation
Public Goods (free rider)Direct public provision, taxation
Information AsymmetryDisclosure requirements, licensing
Pollution / CommonsCap-and-trade permits, standards

6. "There are different difficulties for applying assets revaluation as tax incentive especially at the developing countries." Take-Home 2 pts

TRUE

  • Lack of active, efficient markets to establish fair market values
  • Absence of standardized valuation methodologies and qualified appraisers
  • High inflation complicates depreciation calculations after revaluation
  • Weak institutional capacity to audit and verify revaluation claims
  • Risk of manipulation — firms inflate asset values to claim excessive depreciation and reduce taxable income
  • Developing countries often lack the legal framework to enforce consistent application

7. "According to progressive tax by classes and by brackets, each taxpayer will be subject to different tax rates." Take-Home 2 pts

FALSE

Justification:

  • Progressive by brackets: TRUE — income is divided into multiple brackets, each taxed at a different (increasing) rate. The taxpayer faces several rates. ✓
  • Progressive by classes: FALSE — the taxpayer's entire income is taxed at one single rate determined by the class their total income falls in. Only one rate applies. ✗

Therefore the statement is only true for brackets, not for classes → the overall statement is FALSE as a general claim.

8. "The existence of asymmetric information in the insurance market may lead to market failure." Take-Home 2 pts

TRUE

Justification: Asymmetric information in insurance markets leads to two types of market failure:

  • Adverse selection: High-risk individuals are more likely to buy insurance than low-risk individuals (hidden characteristics). Insurers cannot distinguish them and price accordingly, leading to a market where only high-risk individuals remain — "lemons problem."
  • Moral hazard: After purchasing insurance, insured parties take on more risk (hidden actions) because they bear less financial consequence. This raises costs and premiums for everyone.
  • Both problems can cause insurance markets to collapse (Akerlof's market for lemons) — a clear market failure requiring government intervention (mandatory insurance, risk pooling).

9. "Suppose that the excludability degree of a new service is 0.5 and rivalry degree is 0.6 of this service, so this service should be provided by the private sector only." Take-Home 2 pts

FALSE

Justification: On the goods classification matrix:

  • Excludability = 0.5 (moderate), Rivalry = 0.6 (above mid-point)
  • The service falls in the Common Resource or near-private quadrant — NOT a pure private good
  • With moderate excludability (0.5), it is not fully excludable → free-rider problem exists
  • With rivalry = 0.6, congestion effects occur
  • Conclusion: This good is a quasi-public or common resource, which may require government regulation or co-provision — purely private provision alone would be inefficient

10. "Government budget deficit in Egypt is mainly a structural deficit not a cyclical deficit." Take-Home 2 pts

TRUE

Justification:

  • Cyclical deficit: Caused by economic downturns when tax revenues fall and automatic stabilizer spending rises. It reverses when the economy recovers.
  • Structural deficit: Persists regardless of the business cycle — caused by permanent imbalances between government expenditures and revenues embedded in fiscal laws and obligations.
  • Egypt's deficit has persisted for decades across all economic cycles, driven by structural factors: large public wage bill, fuel and food subsidies, interest payments on high public debt (Ch.3 is 800B LE in this exam), and low tax efficiency.
  • This makes it primarily structural, requiring deep fiscal reforms rather than just waiting for economic recovery.

11. "The government price guarantee policy (price support) in the agricultural market achieves economic efficiency." Take-Home 3 pts

FALSE

Justification:

  • If the free market is already efficient (no externalities, no market failure), government price support above the equilibrium price creates inefficiency (DWL):
  • Producers over-produce beyond the socially optimal quantity
  • Government must purchase the surplus at a cost, creating a fiscal burden with no social gain
  • Price support is only justified when there is a market failure (e.g., farmers cannot access credit, price instability creates poverty). In a competitive, efficient market, it violates the first welfare theorem.
This connects directly to Question 4 below, which provides a numerical example of this analysis.

Fourth Question 10 Points

In the competitive wheat market, the free equilibrium price is 45 LE, and at this price the quantity produced and consumed is 100 tons. If the government guarantees a price of 50 LE for each ton of wheat, the consumer price drops by 5 LE below the free market price, and the quantity produced / consumed increases by 50 units. Determine the effects on consumer surplus, producer surplus, government budget, and economic efficiency.

Given Data:

VariableBefore GuaranteeAfter Price Guarantee
Price paid by Consumer45 LE40 LE (dropped by 5 LE)
Price received by Producer45 LE50 LE (guaranteed)
Quantity (tons)100 tons150 tons
Key Observation: The free market was at equilibrium P=45, Q=100 — this was the efficient (socially optimal) point. Government intervention at P=50 guarantee creates a price wedge between what producers receive and consumers pay.

A) Effect on Consumer Surplus

Consumer price falls from 45 LE to 40 LE, quantity rises from 100 to 150 tons.
Consumer surplus INCREASES.

Consumers pay less (40 LE instead of 45 LE) and buy more (150 tons instead of 100). The area of consumer surplus triangle expands significantly.

B) Effect on Producer Surplus

Producer price rises from 45 LE to 50 LE, quantity rises from 100 to 150 tons.
Producer surplus INCREASES.

Producers receive more (50 LE instead of 45 LE) and sell more (150 tons instead of 100). Producer surplus expands.

C) Government Budget Cost

Government Subsidy per unit = Producer Price − Consumer Price
= 50 − 40 = 10 LE per ton

Total Government Cost = Subsidy per unit × Quantity
= 10 × 150 = 1,500 LE
Government budget cost = 1,500 LE (fiscal burden)

D) Effect on Economic Efficiency (DWL)

The original free market equilibrium (Q=100, P=45) WAS efficient.
Government pushes output to Q=150, which is ABOVE the efficient level.

Deadweight Loss = area of DWL triangle
DWL = ½ × (Q_new − Q_eff) × (P_producer − P_consumer)
DWL = ½ × (150 − 100) × (50 − 40)
DWL = ½ × 50 × 10 = 250 LE
Efficiency Loss (DWL) = 250 LE
The policy creates overproduction beyond the socially optimal level. Resources are misallocated — society is worse off by the DWL amount.

Conclusion: Since the free market was already at the efficient equilibrium (no externalities, no market failure), the price guarantee is NOT economically justified. It creates a deadweight loss of 250 LE and costs the government 1,500 LE while the benefits (CS gain + PS gain) are less than the total cost. The policy is a net welfare loss for society — justification could only exist if there were market failures such as price instability, farmer income vulnerability, or food security concerns.

Topic A: Monopolistic Practices and Their Effects 5 pts

Monopolistic practices occur when a firm with market power restricts competition to maintain dominance.

Types of Monopolistic Practices:

  • Price fixing: Colluding with competitors to set prices above competitive levels (cartel behavior)
  • Predatory pricing: Temporarily pricing below cost to drive out competitors, then raising prices
  • Exclusive dealing: Forcing suppliers/distributors not to deal with competitors
  • Tying arrangements: Requiring buyers of one product to also buy another
  • Mergers and acquisitions: Acquiring competitors to reduce market competition
  • Abuse of dominance: Using market power to discriminate in pricing across customers

Effects of Monopolistic Practices:

Affected PartyEffect
ConsumersHigher prices, reduced quantity, lower consumer surplus, fewer choices
ProducersSupernormal profits for monopolist; smaller firms are harmed or driven out
SocietyDeadweight loss — resources misallocated, social welfare below competitive optimum
InnovationMixed effect: monopoly profits may fund R&D, but lack of competition reduces pressure to innovate
EconomyReduced efficiency, potential for rent-seeking behavior, wealth transfers from consumers to monopolist

Government Response:

  • Anti-monopoly legislation (competition law)
  • Price regulation for natural monopolies (public utilities)
  • Nationalization in extreme cases
  • Breaking up monopolies (structural remedies)

Topic B: Assumptions and Conditions of the Free Market System 5 pts

The free market theory rests on several idealized assumptions that must hold for markets to achieve efficiency:

1. Perfect Competition:

  • Many buyers and sellers — no single agent can influence price (price takers)
  • Homogeneous products — goods are identical, perfect substitutes
  • Free entry and exit — no barriers to entering or leaving the market

2. Perfect Information:

  • All buyers and sellers have complete, accurate information about prices, quality, and opportunities
  • No information asymmetry between parties

3. No Externalities:

  • All costs and benefits are fully captured in market prices
  • No third-party effects from production or consumption decisions

4. Well-Defined Property Rights:

  • All goods are private — excludable and rivalrous
  • Property rights are clearly defined, enforced, and transferable

5. No Public Goods:

  • All goods are private — no free-rider problems

6. Rational Behavior:

  • Consumers maximize utility; firms maximize profits
  • All agents make rational decisions based on self-interest

Significance:

When these conditions hold, the First Welfare Theorem guarantees that market equilibrium is Pareto efficient. When conditions fail (externalities, public goods, monopoly, asymmetric information) → market failure → government intervention justified.

Topic C: Reasons and Causes of Market Failure 5 pts

Market failure occurs when the free market fails to allocate resources efficiently, leading to a loss of social welfare.

1. Externalities:

  • Negative externalities (e.g., pollution): Producers do not bear full social cost → overproduction → DWL → corrective tax needed
  • Positive externalities (e.g., education, vaccination): Consumers do not capture full social benefit → underproduction → DWL → subsidy needed

2. Public Goods:

  • Goods that are non-excludable (cannot prevent free-riders) and non-rivalrous (one person's use does not reduce availability)
  • Private market will not provide them (or underprovide) because firms cannot recover costs from free-riders
  • Examples: national defense, lighthouses, public health

3. Monopoly / Imperfect Competition:

  • A firm with market power restricts output and raises prices above marginal cost
  • Creates deadweight loss and wealth transfer from consumers to producer
  • Natural monopoly (e.g., utilities) justifies government regulation

4. Asymmetric Information:

  • Adverse selection: Before a transaction — better-informed party exploits information advantage (e.g., used car market, insurance)
  • Moral hazard: After a transaction — changes in behavior when risk is borne by others (e.g., insurance fraud, excessive risk-taking)

5. Missing Markets:

  • Some goods/services have no markets (e.g., clean air, biodiversity)
  • Without prices, there is no mechanism to ensure efficient allocation

Government Responses Summary:

Positive externalitySubsidy, direct provision
Public goodGovernment provision, taxation
MonopolyAnti-trust, price cap, nationalization
Asymmetric infoDisclosure, licensing, mandatory coverage

Topic D: Tax Holidays in Egypt — Merits, Demerits, and Evaluation 5 pts

Definition:

A tax holiday is a tax incentive granting complete or partial exemption from income tax for a specified period (typically 5–10 years) to attract investment to specific sectors or regions.

Application in Egypt:

Egypt's Investment Law and Free Zone Law have historically provided tax holidays of 5–10 years for investments in new industrial cities, Upper Egypt, remote areas, and export-oriented industries.

Merits (Advantages):

  • Attracts Foreign Direct Investment (FDI): Increases capital inflows, technology transfer, and job creation
  • Regional development: Directs investment to underdeveloped areas (Upper Egypt, Sinai) that would otherwise be overlooked by private capital
  • Employment generation: New businesses create jobs, reducing unemployment
  • Export promotion: Reduces costs for export-oriented industries, improving international competitiveness
  • Easy to administer: Simple rule (zero tax for X years) requires less complex tax administration than other incentives

Demerits (Disadvantages):

  • Revenue cost: Forgone tax revenue reduces government capacity to fund public services and infrastructure
  • Short-term investment: Investors may depart when the holiday expires — no long-run commitment to the host country
  • Inequity: Existing businesses (paying full taxes) compete at a disadvantage against tax-exempt newcomers
  • Abuse and tax avoidance: Investors may artificially shift profits to tax holiday periods (e.g., using related-party transactions)
  • Misallocation: Without sector constraints, investment may flow to already-profitable sectors that don't need incentives
  • Compared to accelerated depreciation: Tax holidays give equal benefit to profitable and marginal investments — accelerated depreciation is more targeted (only benefits profitable investments)

Evaluation:

Tax holidays are most effective when: (1) they target clearly-defined underdeveloped regions, (2) they are time-limited with clear graduation, (3) they are conditioned on employment thresholds or local content requirements, and (4) they are accompanied by strong anti-avoidance rules. Without these constraints, they are a costly and inefficient policy tool — revenue is forgone without commensurate long-run investment benefits. For Egypt, the shift toward more targeted incentives (such as accelerated depreciation and investment tax credits) is generally preferable to blanket tax holidays.
