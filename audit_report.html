2. Lecture 1: State Budget in Egypt

Content Summary

This lecture covers the Egyptian state budget: definitions, types, classification systems, and deficit measurement. It uses the 2019/2020 fiscal year as a worked example.

Key Concepts

  1. Definition of State Budget — Classical (financial document) vs. Keynesian (financial plan with societal goals: economic growth, social equity, economic stability)
  2. Types of State Budget — Classical (line-item) vs. Modern: Program & Performance, Zero Base, Planning & Programming
  3. Classification Systems — Economical vs. Functional (Egypt uses economical; also required to submit functional per Law 87/2005)
  4. 5 Revenue Chapters: (1) Taxes, (2) Grants & Gifts, (3) Non-tax Revenues, (4) Financial Asset Sales, (5) Government Borrowing
  5. 8 Expenditure Chapters: (1) Wages & Salaries, (2) Purchases of Goods & Services, (3) Interest on Loans, (4) Subsidies & Social Benefits, (5) Other Expenses, (6) Investments, (7) Purchases of Financial Assets, (8) Loan Repayments
  6. Sub-classification: Current (Ch.1-5 Exp; Ch.1-3 Rev) • Capital (Ch.6 Exp; Ch.4 Rev) • Financial Appropriation (Ch.7-8 Exp; Ch.5 Rev)
  7. Three Deficit Types:
    • Cash Deficit = ΣRev(Ch.1+2+3) − ΣExp(Ch.1+2+3+4+5+6)
    • Total Deficit = Cash Deficit + (Ch.4 Rev − Ch.7 Exp)
    • Primary Deficit = Total Deficit − Interest Payments (Ch.3 Exp)

3. Lecture 2: Externalities & Government Policy

Content Summary

This lecture covers externalities classification, how they prevent market efficiency, corrective taxes and subsidies, monopoly with externalities, pollution permits, and carbon tax policy.

Key Concepts

  1. Externalities — Costs/benefits of market transactions not reflected in prices, borne by third parties
  2. Negative Externalities — External costs (pollution, noise); MSC = MPC + MEC
  3. Positive Externalities — External benefits (vaccination, education); MSB = MPB + MEB
  4. Pecuniary Externalities — Effects of price changes on existing consumers from demand/supply shifts
  5. Efficiency condition: MSC = MSB
  6. Corrective Tax = MEC (internalises negative externality; shifts supply up)
  7. Corrective Subsidy = MEB (internalises positive externality; shifts demand up)
  8. Monopoly with Externalities — Two distortions in opposite directions; may partially or fully offset each other
  9. Pollution Permits — Equivalent to corrective taxes (set quantity, price determined by market)
  10. Carbon Tax — Real-world application to reduce CO2 emissions

Key Formulas

Efficiency: MSC = MSBSocial optimum

4. Lecture 3: Introduction to Public Finance

Content Summary

This 108-page lecture covers the consequences of government finance, taxation principles (Classical vs. Keynesian), tax base categories, tax rate structures (proportional, progressive, regressive), tax burden distribution, and alternatives to taxation.

Key Concepts

  1. Consequences of Government Finance:
    • Political equilibrium (tax shares affect voting choices)
    • Market equilibrium & efficiency (finance methods distort prices)
    • Income distribution
  2. Classical vs. Keynesian Tax Theory:
    • Classical: Tax serves only financial purpose; free market achieves equity/stability
    • Keynesian: Tax achieves financial, economic, and social goals; compulsory payments without direct return
  3. Tax Base (3 categories):
    • (1) Income (direct tax): profits, salaries, rents, interest
    • (2) Wealth (direct tax): accumulated savings & investment
    • (3) Consumption/Sales (indirect tax): VAT, customs, sales tax
  4. General vs. Selective Taxes:
    • General: all components of base, no exclusions, uniform rate
    • Selective: certain items only, with exemptions (excise, real estate, profit taxes)
  5. Tax Rate Structure:
    • ATR = Total Tax / Tax Base (Income)
    • MTR = ΔT / ΔY

Tax Rate Types

TypeATR vs MTRKey Feature
Proportional (no exemption) ATR = MTR (constant) T = t(Y); flat tax; both rates equal and constant
Proportional (with exemption) MTR > ATR always T = −a + t(Y); ATR rises with income but never reaches MTR
Progressive by Brackets MTR > ATR always Income split into brackets; each bracket has higher rate; MTR constant within bracket
Progressive by Classes ATR = MTR (within class) Entire income taxed at one rate based on class; can cause ranking reversals
Regressive by Brackets ATR > MTR Tax rate decreases with higher brackets; rewards higher productivity
Regressive by Classes ATR = MTR (within class) Higher income class → lower rate; against social equity

Tax Burden Distribution

PrincipleDescriptionChallenges
Benefit Principle Tax proportional to benefit received from public goods Free rider problem; hard to measure marginal benefit; only practical for small communities or excludable goods (tolls, fares)
Ability to Pay Principle Tax proportional to economic capacity (income/wealth) Requires Horizontal Equity (same capacity → same tax) and Vertical Equity (different capacity → different tax). Satisfied by progressive taxation.

Alternatives to Taxes

AlternativeDescription
DonationsVoluntary contributions; limited impact in developing countries
User ChargesFees, tolls, fares for excludable public services (highways, bridges, recreation)
Public PricesPrices for goods from public enterprises (electricity, water); set at average cost + normal return
Public DebtInternal or external borrowing; government issues bonds; affects intergenerational well-being
Inflationary FinancePrinting money / expanding money supply; causes inflation especially if supply is inelastic
