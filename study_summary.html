1. State Budget in Egypt

Definitions

Classical View

A financial document representing expected revenues and expenditures, authorized by the legislation authority. Budget should be at the minimum level.

Keynesian View

A financial plan for government activities reflecting predictions for revenues and expenses, to achieve society's targets (economic growth, social equity, economic stability).

Budget Types

  • Classical Budget: Line-item (items by items)
  • Modern Budget (3 types):
    1. Program & Performance Budget
    2. Zero Base Budget
    3. Planning & Programming Budget

Egypt’s Classification System

Egypt uses the economical classification. Also required to submit functional classification per Law 87/2005 (compatible with UN and OECD standards).

2. Budget Deficit Types

1. Cash Deficit = Σ Rev (Ch.1 + Ch.2 + Ch.3) − Σ Exp (Ch.1 + Ch.2 + Ch.3 + Ch.4 + Ch.5 + Ch.6)
2. Total Deficit = Cash Deficit + Net Financial Assets
   where Net Financial Assets = Ch.4Rev − Ch.7Exp
3. Primary Deficit = Total Deficit − Interest Payments (Ch.3Exp)
Exam Tip: If Primary Deficit is negative, it means there is a Primary Surplus. This means the government earns enough to cover all expenses except debt interest.

3. Externalities — Overview

Externalities are costs or benefits of market transactions not reflected in market prices, but affecting third parties other than buyers and sellers.

Negative Externalities

External costs borne by third parties.

  • Pollution (air, water, noise)
  • Traffic congestion
  • Smoking health effects

Result: Market overproduces; price is too low.

Positive Externalities

External benefits enjoyed by third parties.

  • Vaccination
  • Education
  • Smoke alarms / fireproofing

Result: Market underproduces; price is too high.

Core Formulas

MSC = MPC + MEC    (Marginal Social Cost)
MSB = MPB + MEB    (Marginal Social Benefit)

Market Equilibrium: MPB = MPC   (ignores externalities)
Efficient Point: MSC = MSB   (accounts for externalities)
Pecuniary Externalities: Effects of price changes on existing consumers from demand/supply shifts. These are not true externalities because they work through the price system.

4. Negative Externalities & Corrective Tax

The Problem

  • MPC reflects supply, but MSC > MPC because external costs are not priced
  • Market equilibrium at E1 (MPB = MPC): quantity q1 is too high, price P1 is too low
  • Efficient point at E2 (MSB = MSC): quantity q2 < q1, price P2 > P1
  • Deadweight loss = triangle E1E2E3

The Solution: Corrective Tax

Corrective Tax per unit = MEC (Marginal External Cost)
  • Tax shifts supply curve up from S1 (MPC) to S2 (MSC)
  • New equilibrium = efficient point E2
  • Consumers pay more (P2) and consume less (q2)
  • Producers receive less (P3 = P2 − tax)

Paper Industry Example

Before TaxAfter Tax
Quantity150100 (efficient)
Market Price$50$55
Producer Price$50$45

Tax Revenue Uses

  1. Compensate affected third parties
  2. Improve government services
  3. Reduce other taxes
Important: Corrective tax does not reduce pollution to zero. It raises the cost of using environmental resources to reflect marginal damage. Producers then choose among: (a) recycling waste, (b) purifying waste, (c) paying the tax, or (d) exiting the industry.

5. Positive Externalities & Corrective Subsidy

The Problem

  • MSB > MPB because external benefits are not captured by the market
  • Market equilibrium at E1 (MPB = MPC): quantity q1 is too low, price too high
  • Efficient point at E2 (MSB = MSC): quantity q2 > q1
  • Efficiency loss = triangle E1E2E3

The Solution: Corrective Subsidy

Corrective Subsidy per unit = MEB (Marginal External Benefit)
  • Subsidy shifts demand curve up from MPB to MSB
  • New equilibrium = efficient point E2
  • Consumers pay less (P3) and consume more (q2)
  • Producers receive more (P2) and produce more (q2)
  • Efficiency gain = triangle E1E2E3
Total Subsidy Cost = q2 × (P2 − P3) = efficient quantity × subsidy per unit

Constant vs. Decreasing MEB

AssumptionDescriptionImpact
Constant MEB MEB is the same at every output level MSB shifts parallel above MPB
Decreasing MEB (realistic) MEB declines with more output; becomes zero at point q3 MSB converges toward MPB; beyond q3, MSB = MPB

6. Monopoly with Externalities

Two Distortions in Opposite Directions

DistortionEffect on Output
Monopolistic powerReduces output below efficient level
Negative externalityPushes output above efficient level

Three Possible Outcomes

ScenarioConditionPolicy
Distortions exactly offset Monopolist output = efficient output No intervention needed
Monopolist overproduces (Fig 10-a) Efficient output < monopolist output Corrective tax (but less than under perfect competition)
Monopolist underproduces (Fig 10-b) Efficient output > monopolist output Corrective subsidy (not tax!)
Exam Tip: The corrective tax under monopoly is always less than the corrective tax required under perfect competition for the same externality, because the monopoly distortion already reduces output.

7. Taxation Principles

Classical Theory

  • Tax serves only financial purpose
  • Should be at minimum level
  • Free market achieves equity and stability without government

Keynesian Theory

  • Tax achieves financial, economic, and social goals
  • Compulsory payments without direct return
  • Used to reallocate resources and redistribute purchasing power
  • Revenue used for: (1) transfer payments/social programs, (2) purchasing inputs for public goods

9. Tax Rate Structure

Key Definitions

ATR (Average Tax Rate) = T / Y = Total Tax Revenue / Tax Base (Income)

MTR (Marginal Tax Rate) = ΔT / ΔY = Change in Tax / Change in Income

Proportional Tax

A. Without Exemption (Flat Tax)

T = t × Y
  • ATR = MTR = t (constant at all income levels)
  • Total tax curve: positive straight line from origin

B. With Exemption

T = −a + t × Y   (where a = exempted income × t)
  • ATR = (−a/Y) + t   →   ATR < MTR always
  • MTR = t (constant)
  • ATR rises with income but never equals MTR
  • Below exemption level: person may receive subsidy
Exam Tip: A proportional tax with exemption is sometimes mistaken for progressive. It is still proportional because the marginal rate is constant. However, the average rate does rise — which makes it effectively progressive in its average burden.

Progressive Tax

A. By Brackets

  • Income divided into brackets, each with a higher rate
  • A taxpayer's income may be subject to multiple rates
  • MTR > ATR at all brackets (except first where both = 0%)
  • MTR is constant within a bracket; increases when moving to higher bracket
  • ATR rises steadily with income

Example (Table 2 from lecture)

BracketIncome RangeTax Rate (MTR)
B1$0 – $5,0000%
B2$5,001 – $10,00010%
B3$10,001 – $20,00020%
B4$20,001 – $40,00040%
B5Above $40,00050%

For income = $100,000: Total tax = 0 + 500 + 2,000 + 8,000 + 30,000 = $40,500. ATR = 40,500/100,000 = 40.5%. MTR = 50%.

B. By Classes

  • Taxpayer classified into one class based on total income
  • Entire income taxed at that class’s rate
  • ATR = MTR (within each class)
  • Problem: Can cause ranking reversals (person with slightly higher income ends up with less after-tax income)
Progressive by brackets is preferred by most countries because it avoids the ranking reversal problem of progressive by classes, and more effectively achieves social equity.

Regressive Tax

  • Tax rate decreases as income increases
  • By brackets: ATR > MTR (opposite of progressive); highest bracket has lowest rate
  • By classes: ATR = MTR within class; higher class = lower rate
  • Against social equity: lower-income pays proportionally more
  • Real-world example: US payroll tax (Social Security) — 15.3% up to $65,400, then 2.9% above

10. Tax Burden Distribution

Benefit Principle

Each person pays tax proportional to benefit received from public goods & services.

Best measure: Marginal benefit

Challenges:

  • Free rider problem
  • Hard to measure marginal benefit from pure public goods
  • No incentive for individuals to reveal true benefits
  • Only practical in small communities

Applications: Gasoline tax → roads; tolls; public transport fares; admission fees

Ability to Pay Principle

Each person pays tax proportional to economic capacity.

Two equity rules:

  • Horizontal Equity: Same capacity → same tax
  • Vertical Equity: Different capacity → different tax

Measure of capacity: Income (most common), wealth, or combination

Equal sacrifice principle: Rich pay more because marginal benefit of money decreases with wealth

Best tool: Progressive taxation

11. Alternatives to Taxes

AlternativeDescriptionKey Points
1. Donations Voluntary contributions to government Used for disaster relief, wartime; very limited in developing countries
2. User Charges Prices set by political decision for excludable services Forms: Fees (licenses), Tolls (roads/bridges), Fares (transport). Cannot finance pure public goods (non-excludable).
3. Public Prices Prices for goods from public enterprises (electricity, water) Set at average cost + normal return on capital. Common in socialist economies.
4. Public Debt Government borrowing (internal or external) Internal: from citizens (bonds). External: from other countries. Affects intergenerational well-being.
5. Inflationary Finance Printing money / expanding money supply Government borrows from central bank. Main effect: inflation. Especially harmful if supply is inelastic.
Exam Tip: User charges cannot finance pure public goods because the exclusion principle doesn't apply to them (non-excludable + non-rivalrous).

12. Master Formula Sheet

Externalities

MSC = MPC + MEC
MSB = MPB + MEB
Efficiency: MSC = MSB
Market Equilibrium: MPB = MPC
Corrective Tax = MEC
Corrective Subsidy = MEB
Total Subsidy = qefficient × subsidy per unit
Deadweight Loss = ½ × ΔQ × MEC (for constant MEC, triangular area)

State Budget Deficits

Cash Deficit = Σ Rev (Ch.1+2+3) − Σ Exp (Ch.1+2+3+4+5+6)
Total Deficit = Cash Deficit + (Ch.4Rev − Ch.7Exp)
Primary Deficit = Total Deficit − Ch.3Exp (Interest)
Interest as % of Current Exp = Ch.3Exp / Σ Exp(Ch.1–6) × 100

Tax Rate Structure

ATR = T / Y
MTR = ΔT / ΔY

Proportional (no exemption): T = tY → ATR = MTR = t
Proportional (with exemption): T = −a + tY → ATR = (−a/Y) + t < MTR = t

Progressive by brackets: MTR > ATR (always); MTR constant within bracket
Progressive by classes: MTR = ATR (within class); entire income at one rate

13. True/False Exam Preparation

The 2025 exams featured 10 True/False statements each. Below are the key topics and common traps:

Frequently Tested Statements

#TopicKey Facts to Remember
1 Budget views Classical: minimum financial document. Keynesian: tool for economic/social goals.
2 Proportional tax Without exemption: ATR = MTR. With exemption: MTR > ATR.
3 Progressive tax by brackets vs classes Brackets: MTR > ATR. Classes: MTR = ATR, but can cause ranking reversals.

15 Egypt classification 5 revenue chapters, 8 expenditure chapters, economical classification.
